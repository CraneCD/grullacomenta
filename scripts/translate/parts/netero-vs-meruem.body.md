Action has always been one of my favorite genres (when it's done well). At its best, it's basically a physical form of storytelling, where the climax of a fight reflects the emotional catharsis of the characters. To an outside observer, having a group of people beat each other up might seem simple, but there's an elegant construction behind any great fight scene. For action to be engaging and interesting, you need goals, stakes, obstacles, and twists, as well as characters who are interesting enough and, in the case of anime, good animation.

That said, in this video I want to analyze one particular fight in Hunter x Hunter that I've been thinking about using those elements: specifically, the fight between Netero and Meruem in the Chimera Ants arc.

Hunter x Hunter has always excelled at constructing fights. Between the creativity and the solid foundation of Nen, this anime's version of the typical Shonen superpower, the series demonstrates time and again a great understanding of the crucial elements of a good fight scene that I was talking about earlier. The fight between Netero and Meruem is no exception, as can be seen in its setup. On Netero's side, the goal of the fight is to eliminate Meruem in order to stop the selection process, and the stakes are the survival of humanity itself. The obstacle, of course, is the fact that Meruem turns out to be far stronger than Netero. With just these factors, as an audience, we already have a clear idea of the conflict. However, once the fight "begins," Meruem... decides to sit down and talk with Netero instead of fighting.

It turns out that, on Meruem's side, what he wanted was not to defeat Netero, but to talk with him in order to better understand humanity. His experience with Komugi had made him reflect on humanity's potential, and he wanted to discuss it further. This is a great example of how Hunter x Hunter establishes the divergent motivations of characters in order to alter the dynamics of a conflict. Knowing and understanding why characters fight not only makes the fight emotionally engaging for the audience, but can also alter its trajectory depending on the circumstances, making it more varied and interesting. Now, on Netero's side, we have a twist that prevents him from reaching his goal: his opponent doesn't even want to fight! In better circumstances, Netero might have been willing to indulge Meruem, but here Netero's secondary stakes come into play: if something bad happens during the extermination of the Chimera Ants, the Hunter Association will be held responsible, and that's why Netero is forced to act.

At first, Netero used physical coercion to force Meruem to fight, but Meruem wouldn't budge. As can be seen at this point, the twist in Netero's goal becomes a conflict of its own, with its own set of goals (getting Meruem to fight) and obstacles (Meruem's imposing strength). However, Netero, and by extension Hunter x Hunter, ends up taking advantage of Meruem's internal struggle, that is, his identity crisis, and turns that character conflict into Meruem's goal for this fight. By telling him that he knows his name, Netero manages to force Meruem to fight. And so the battle begins.

Considering that both Meruem and Netero are the strongest characters the series has shown so far, there was some concern about how the fight would play out. Specifically, I was worried that it would all come down to who could hit harder. But this is where Hunter x Hunter once again demonstrates its understanding of goals and obstacles. Instead of simply being "defeat the other," the series chooses to give the fight a very specific condition or goal for winning. The unique nature of Netero's Nen means that, in theory, he has an unlimited number of moves to make, but Meruem takes his time analyzing the patterns of Netero's moves and using those predictions to attack. Thus, the battle between Netero and Meruem becomes a struggle of endurance, where the goal of both sides is the submission of the other. Whether Netero first submits to his own biases, becoming predictable to Meruem, or Meruem gives up before finding an exploitable pattern. Meruem's goal of extracting his name from Netero also acts as a handicap that levels the playing field a little, because it means Meruem can't really go all out against Netero without running the risk of dying, which makes the fight more tense. Once again, this is an example of how the series uses the characters' goals to alter the variables of the conflict. Although it's still clear that Meruem has more advantages, this additional twist makes the conclusion of the fight a little more uncertain.

The Thematic Conclusion

Unfortunately, despite his best effort, Netero ends up losing to Meruem. But this is where the exploration of humanity in this arc of Hunter x Hunter resurfaces, making the conclusion of the fight thematic. Although Meruem praises Netero's strength, he mentions how his power comes from the culmination of the collective instinct of his species, something humanity will never be able to achieve due to the individuality it possesses. But, to Meruem's horror, Netero reveals that the true strength of humanity's individuality does not lie in how our species can surpass itself physically.

Rather, it lies in our capacity to destroy.

In his final moment, Netero detonates the Poor Man's Rose, exploding alongside Meruem. In any other fight, this would be a triumphant moment of humanity's victory, but the following episode reminds us of the grim reality surrounding the bomb. As can be inferred from the shape of its smoke cloud, the Poor Man's Rose is an intentional evocation of the nuclear bomb of our real world. They are both a symbol of humanity's progress and its capacity to fight existential threats, as well as a mark of our own destructive tendencies. Both in the Hunter x Hunter universe and in the real world, these bombs have claimed millions of lives, all dropped by individuals with political motivations for their own ends. And so, once again, we as viewers are reminded of the duality of humanity: our capacity to defeat "evil" and our capacity to harm and destroy.

These elements are why the fight between Netero and Meruem is a testament to what makes Hunter x Hunter a great action series. Not only does it have a strong command of the fundamentals of conflict (goals, stakes, obstacles, twists, etc.), but it also knows how to end its fights in ways that further elaborate on their underlying themes.
