
A basic element in anime in the action-packed genre aimed at young male audiences is the main protagonist who has the task of saving the world. It depends on them to bring justice where it cannot be found, and most of the time they succeed. But as some series demonstrate, not all protagonists can maintain such a firm grip on their sense of justice. Light Yagami from Death Note, Eren Jaeger from Shingeki no Kyojin, and Lelouch vi Britannia from Code Geass all have something in common: their belief in justice changes and they play at being gods, deciding who and what deserves to be saved and who doesn't.

Each one has their own way of going through this process of saving certain characters. From an external perspective it appears appropriate because, ultimately, they are saving people. However, saving a person always has a price, and each character learns that their actions driven by their divine-type complexes have a broadly negative result.

A God complex is defined as a personality pattern in which an individual believes they have great power or influence over the lives of others and that they are superior to them. It is mentioned in multiple sources that when someone is affected by a God complex, they can become destructive and harmful to those around them. Being able to control a situation by force is a gigantic factor that contributes to this personality pattern, which is rooted in a deep sense of egocentrism.

God complexes take root for a variety of reasons, such as low self-esteem, feelings of helplessness, or in many cases because they have an over-protective and permissive authority figure. This type of thinking cultivates that complex in them through praise that makes them feel special and superior. Not all these patterns have to be present for a character to develop a God complex; one or a couple of them is enough, as we see in our examples. Eren is powerless against the chaos of his world, Light is put on a pedestal by everyone around him, and Lelouch in some cases feels inferior and cannot stand it.

Given these effects in their past, it's possible they don't recognize the impact they have on other people around them, which obviously generates a much bigger problem when the change in their beliefs occurs and they begin to believe that passing judgment on others is their duty.

For those three of you who don't know this legendary anime, Death Note began as a manga that was first released in 2003 and created by Tsugumi Ohba and Takeshi Obata. Its anime adaptation took place between 2006 and 2007. In it we follow our first example: Light Yagami, a high school student who finds a mysterious notebook called the "Death Note," which allows whoever uses it to kill someone simply by writing their name on the notebook's pages.

When Light discovers the Death Note, he sees it as an opportunity to eradicate evil from the world and sets himself on a path to become the God of that new world, and consequently create a place free of injustice and populated only by those who are honest. After his murders gain negative attention from authorities who begin searching for him as a serial killer, Light decides to expose his beliefs to the public, creating an audience that sees him as a cult and that deviates from his final intention of only eradicating evil in order to hide his identity and stay safe.

Of course, this captures the attention of someone called L, a super genius detective who begins a mental battle to capture Kira, the serial killer. At the beginning of Death Note, Light is known for being hardworking and a natural genius. He excels both inside and outside of school, which leads his parents to put him on a very high pedestal as the perfect boy. His need for justice led him to formulate his New World goal, which can be attributed to his father, who is the head of the national police agency.

Having a father who works to keep society in order has taught Light about the injustices that occur day by day and has exposed him to the worst of society. This, combined with the constant praise they give him, allows him to turn that adoration into a weapon. When Light begins murdering criminals and people start to notice, the praise he receives extends beyond what he believed possible. Civilians begin to call him Kira, and by giving him that recognition they make him believe he is "The Chosen One." Light has the responsibility to make the world a better place because, to his knowledge, he is the only person with the powers to end lives by simply writing a name in a notebook.

Turning praise into something negative could be considered narcissism, a personality type very close to the God complex. Narcissists treat praise as a privilege or a right. By putting Light, and consequently Kira, on a pedestal, it was obvious it wouldn't take long for Light to start seeing himself as a God who could determine whether any person, innocent or not, deserved to live.

The pretexts of the greater good that Light had to begin his killing vanish when he takes the lives of two innocent FBI agents, all to protect himself. One of them was the only one to realize Kira's real identity before his death. The reason Light killed these agents was simply to protect himself, since he didn't feel comfortable with the fact that they were pursuing him. His justification for his murders as a necessity takes his narcissism to the extreme, considering the obstacles to the utopian world he was convinced he could create. After all, what are a couple of innocent lives for the common good?

When L enters the equation, Light learns he's not the only genius in the playing field, threatening his fragile ego with someone who truly has a chance to defeat him, and without the ability to kill him like he has. The need to escape unharmed, kill L, and become someone absolute ends up becoming his main motivation, forgetting his original excuses.

After Light is finally able to kill L, he thinks he has won and feels fulfilled as a superior being. However, as we all know, this is just the beginning, since Near, L's successor, is even more intelligent and eventually manages to set a plan in motion to stop Light. This point of stillness for a character who had his way for years was a product of overconfidence. His God complex reached such a level after thinking he had won that it ended in his destruction, leaving nothing more than a pile of dead bodies, including himself. Not a perfect world without evil, but a sad story full of tragedy.

Based on the 2009 manga of the same name written by Hajime Isayama, Shingeki no Kyojin tells the story of Eren Jaeger and his childhood friends Armin Arlert and Mikasa Ackerman, living within the protective walls of Paradis. They live a relatively normal life while outside these walls, man-eating titans lurk.

One day, destruction and tragedy arrive in Eren's world when these walls are infiltrated by a 60-meter-tall colossal titan and an armored titan that destroys the gate separating the humble citizens from the man-eating titans. Hell breaks loose culminating in the death of Eren's mother. This causes our protagonist's main motivation, who swears to take revenge against the titans and enlists to become a member of the survey corps along with Armin and Mikasa to kill them all.

This initial goal evolved completely: from genocidally killing all titans, it also becomes ensuring the safety of his best friends and his people regardless of personal consequences for himself. In this sense, he is completely altruistic. After his mother's death, Eren sets as his first objective to kill all the titans who have terrorized his people in Paradis for years. Of course, this is before being exposed to the real horror of this world.

Eren's main themes are desperation and helplessness. He grew up as an only child in Paradis and didn't have the real authority to make a difference in the events of history until he realized he had inherited the power to become a titan. When he discovered this, he felt he finally had a way to turn his goal into reality. He was eventually classified as humanity's hope, and like Light, was put on a pedestal, which puts the expectations of all humanity on his shoulders, contributing to his eventual descent from hero to villain.

With these expectations, Eren begins to learn more about who he is as a titan and about the history surrounding Paradis's existence. He discovers that his titan is the direct result of interference from the nation across the sea: Marley. Eren discovers this when he unlocks his father's memories, who inherited his titan to him, and with this his original goal of avenging his mother's death goes out the window. As the only one who knows the history behind the two nations, his new goal becomes destroying the Marleyans, who have forced the villagers and population of Paradis into a life of pain and discrimination.

Those who have strong helplessness, also called moral distress as Eren strongly had, feel they must set themselves aside due to external forces. Eren originally hated the titans; however, when he learned he could use them for his new objective, he began to see them as nothing more than a tool, and even became his main weapon, using them to finish off his new enemy.

Eventually a division was created between Eren and the militia, and just like a God, Eren gained a cult of followers who supported all his actions and decisions, even if this meant the mass murder of thousands of innocents. Eren's declaration of war is when his change culminates and he brutally murders Marleyan civilians, just as his mother was murdered, justifying these collateral deaths as absolutely necessary.

This is not the only instance where he shows no compassion for those whose lives he takes. In fact, his plan is to destroy the entire world so that only his loved ones can live truly free. This, of course, is called "The Rumbling," where Eren takes all the power available in this world to release thousands of titans hidden within Paradis's walls to cause mass genocide to everyone except his own people: a God of destruction.

Our last example is the manga written by Ichiro Okouchi and first published in 2006, eventually adapted in the same year. Code Geass tells the story of Lelouch Lamperouge, or Lelouch vi Britannia, and his obsession with destroying the tyrannical Royal Empire of Britannia. This ultra-futuristic version of Japan presents interesting power dynamics and, especially, an ultra-political and fragile world that allows Lelouch to use his superior intelligence and the power of his Geass, which allows him to directly control people, to create a plan to overthrow the Empire.

What's interesting about Lelouch as a character is his secret past, his main motivation being to avenge his mother's death and build an ideal world so his little sister Nunnally can live in peace. Thanks to this motivation, Lelouch becomes the leader of a rebellion against the Empire. But it's not until Nunnally becomes an important figure in the government that he realizes his rebellion is for all people and not just for his family.

Of course, we can't overlook that Lelouch is confident in his intelligence and ability. His self-esteem depends on the people around him, who manage to keep him at a coherent level, at least until Lelouch betrays them, becoming without them a megalomaniac with a God complex. Keep in mind that Lelouch was discarded by his father when he stood against him. That's why I mention he might have low self-esteem in that regard.

What's interesting about Lelouch's God complex is that it's an intermediate between Light and Eren. Light is a complete extreme: without friends, not caring even about his family, everyone is a piece for his eventual goal. Eren, on the contrary, has the same God complex and ends up causing mass genocide, but he does it all to save his friends and family; he never changes this motivation. Lelouch is in the middle: with his friends and family being his main motivation and those who keep him sane after acquiring the power of Geass, but eventually sacrificing almost everyone around him to bring his father down from the throne.

In a sense, Lelouch's God complex breaks him down little by little as a character, turning him into a shell that eventually ends with his tragic death in the arms of his loved ones, realizing that using them was the only mistake he made in his life.

Analyzing these three different but similar characters shows us how different adaptations of the so-called God complex can be. These three examples are quite well-known and obvious, but we can evidence this trait in many characters around anime and manga, and in some of them there isn't even tragedy or nefarious events that occur. It's possible they are more subtle, on a smaller scale, or that they don't even materialize. What is certain is that it's more common than we normally think.

In the end, what I can tell you is that these three characters have given their lives to play at being God and pass judgment as they see fit, and it's equally dazzling and tragic what we see in their stories.
